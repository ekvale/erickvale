Population Health Data Infrastructure

About

Eric Kvale is a data scientist and full-stack developer working at the intersection of clinical informatics, federated data infrastructure, and public health surveillance. His work focuses on building the technical systems that allow public health agencies, health system research consortia, and community organizations to generate timely, accurate health data for populations that existing surveillance systems routinely miss — including people experiencing homelessness, incarceration, and housing instability.

The demonstration environment on this site — including the HTAC pipeline walkthrough, the OMOP common data model implementation, and the privacy-preserving record linkage simulation — represents a working implementation of the federated population health data architecture used by the Minnesota Electronic Health Record Consortium and similar statewide collaboratives. It is built on open-source, interoperable infrastructure: the OMOP CDM, OHDSI tooling, and a Django-based data product layer. The implementation reflects real architectural decisions about governance, small-cell suppression, deduplication, and administrative data linkage that practitioners building similar systems face in the field.

Prior to this practice, Eric worked across nonprofit, public-sector, and collaborative data settings, with a consistent focus on making health data infrastructure work for the communities most affected by health disparities and least represented in the systems designed to measure them.

What This Site Demonstrates

Working OMOP CDM Implementation

The pipeline walkthrough steps through a live federated data environment — from OMOP domain tables and concept mapping through PPRL tokenization, deduplication, administrative data enrichment, cohort queries, and small-cell suppression to stratified prevalence estimates.

Privacy-Preserving Record Linkage

The site implements HMAC-SHA256 token generation for cross-site patient deduplication — the same algorithm validated for public health surveillance use at 97% precision and 75% recall in published multi-site deployments.

Governance-Aware Architecture

Data use agreement alignment, suppression policy, stratification dimensions, and the separation between contributor-held data and published aggregates are built into the system design, not treated as afterthoughts.

Health Equity Orientation

Stratification by race, ethnicity, language, age, sex, homelessness, incarceration, and Medicaid enrollment is a core design requirement, not an optional layer — reflecting the populations this infrastructure is designed to make visible.
