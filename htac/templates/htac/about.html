Health Trends Across Communities (HTAC)

A privacy-preserving statewide surveillance platform that links clinical, administrative, and social-services data to produce suppressed prevalence estimates for over 70 health conditions across Minnesota communities — covering 94% of the state population.

What is HTAC?

HTAC is a collaboration between the Minnesota Electronic Health Record Consortium (MN EHR Consortium / MNEHRC) and state and local public health agencies, funded by the Minnesota Department of Health (MDH) through a CDC Public Health Infrastructure Grant. It answers a foundational question in public health surveillance: how common is a health condition across different communities in Minnesota?

Traditional surveillance relies on death certificates, disease registries, or voluntary surveys — each with significant coverage gaps. HTAC instead aggregates electronic health records (EHRs) from 11 large health systems that together serve more than 90% of Minnesotans, de-duplicates patients who appear at more than one site, and joins to administrative data (Medicaid, corrections, housing, immunizations, vital statistics) to capture the full picture of a person's health history.

HTAC uses a federated distributed architecture — query scripts run against each site's local OMOP database and only aggregated, de-identified summary counts are transmitted to the Data Coordinating Center (DCC). No patient-level data ever leaves a participating health system.

What is the MN EHR Consortium?

The Minnesota Electronic Health Record Consortium (MNEHRC) is a public-private collaboration established in 2020, initially to support COVID-19 pandemic surveillance. It now encompasses 11 of the state's largest health systems, public health departments, and other cross-sector partners.

Governance

A Governance Board (one voting member per health system) oversees the consortium. An Executive Committee manages day-to-day operations. A Scientific Review Committee approves all project and manuscript proposals.

Data infrastructure

Each site maintains its own OMOP database. The Technical Assistance Core distributes R query scripts, maintains deduplication procedures, and runs data quality assessments using OHDSI tools (Achilles, Data Quality Dashboard).

Analysis

Condition codesets are defined using OMOP concept IDs. R scripts run at each site and aggregated results are sent to the study Coordinating Site for final analysis and publication.

Equity focus

Estimates are stratified by race, ethnicity, language, geography, age, sex, homelessness, incarceration, and Medicaid status to surface disparities that aggregate data obscure.

Participating health systems: Allina Health · CentraCare · Children's Minnesota · Essentia Health · HealthPartners · Hennepin Healthcare · M Health Fairview · Mayo Clinic · North Memorial Health · Sanford Health · Minneapolis VA Health Care System

What is OMOP?

OMOP (Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership) is an open, internationally adopted common data model (CDM) maintained by the OHDSI collaborative that standardizes how clinical data from different EHR systems are structured and coded.

Without a common model, comparing data from Epic at Allina Health to Oracle Health at Mayo Clinic requires custom translation for every pair of systems. OMOP solves this by giving each participating site a standard schema — the same table names, column names, and vocabulary codes everywhere. MNEHRC spent two years implementing and harmonizing OMOP across all 11 sites before HTAC queries could run consistently.

OMOP domain What it captures Example HTAC use
ConditionOccurrence Diagnoses (ICD-10 mapped to SNOMED concept IDs) Identify patients with Type 2 Diabetes or Opioid Use Disorder
Measurement Lab results and vitals (LOINC codes) HbA1c > 6.5% to refine a diabetes cohort
DrugExposure Prescriptions and administrations (RxNorm) Buprenorphine prescriptions for OUD treatment cohort
Observation Clinical facts outside other domains (SNOMED) Tobacco use, pregnancy status, social determinants
VisitOccurrence Encounters — inpatient, outpatient, ED, telehealth Restrict cohort to patients with ≥1 qualifying visit in study period
Person Demographics — birth year, sex, race, ethnicity, ZIP, census tract Stratify prevalence estimates by race/ethnicity, county, or census tract

Codesets (lists of OMOP concept_ids) are versioned in the HTAC Condition and ConceptCode models so analysts can build and audit condition definitions without touching raw SQL.

How the HTAC Pipeline Works

Walk through each step in detail — data fields captured, why the step exists, and how it works. Start pipeline walkthrough →
  1. Clinical data in OMOP CDM Each site's EHR system maps patient records to the OMOP Common Data Model — a shared vocabulary covering six domains (Person, Visit, Condition, Drug, Measurement, Observation). Query scripts run against each site's local OMOP database; no patient records leave the site.
  2. Tokenization at the site Each health system hashes six PII fields — first name, last name, date of birth, sex, phone number, and ZIP code — into a one-way HMAC-SHA256 token using a shared salt. No raw PII is transmitted. The algorithm has been validated at 97% precision and 75% recall for public health surveillance purposes.
  3. Deduplication into a statewide roster The Data Coordinating Center (DCC) receives tokens from all sites. Because the same PII produces the same hash, patients seen at multiple sites generate identical tokens. deduplicate_tokens() merges these into a single DeduplicatedRoster row. In practice, about 75% of MNEHRC patients have records at more than one health system.
  4. Enrichment with administrative data MDH provides the DCC with periodic extracts from state administrative systems. The roster is joined by token hash to Medicaid enrollment, HMIS (housing/homelessness), DOC (corrections), MIIC (immunizations), and vital statistics — adding social-risk flags (Medicaid-enrolled, unstably housed, incarcerated, deceased) to each entry.
  5. Federated condition cohort queries Analysts define a health condition using OMOP concept codes. Query scripts run at each site against that site's local OMOP database, searching ConditionOccurrence, Measurement, DrugExposure, and Observation tables during a defined study period. Only aggregate counts — not patient records — leave each site.
  6. Stratification and suppression Counts are stratified by geography (state, county, ZIP code, census tract), race/ethnicity, language, age group, sex, and social-risk flags (homelessness, incarceration, Medicaid). Any stratum with fewer than 11 individuals is suppressed before results are stored, per the MNEHRC Master Data Use Agreement. Prevalence rates are calculated per 10,000 enrolled patients.
  7. Reporting Suppressed, stratified estimates are available via the public HTAC dashboard, a public-use data file request, the MDH Admin interface, CSV export, and the HTAC REST API at /api/htac/v1/. Data are updated annually.

Known Data Gaps

Tribal health systems — Minnesota's 11 federally recognized tribal nations operate sovereign health systems under the Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act. Tribal health data are excluded from HTAC unless explicitly authorized through nation-to-nation data-sharing agreements. Prevalence estimates therefore undercount conditions in American Indian / Alaska Native communities.

Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) and community clinics — Most FQHCs and some rural independent clinics serving low-income, uninsured, and immigrant populations are not yet integrated (~10% of MN patients). Because these sites disproportionately serve people without commercial insurance, their absence creates undercount bias in estimates for low-income and non-English-speaking communities.

Privacy & Governance

  • PPRL tokens are one-way cryptographic hashes — mathematically irreversible without the shared salt.
  • Small-cell suppression (n < 11) is applied to all strata before any estimate is stored or published, per the MNEHRC Master DUA.
  • Patient-level data never leaves a participating health system — only aggregate counts are transmitted to the DCC.
  • Data use is governed by the MNEHRC Master Data Use Agreement and project-specific appendices.
  • HTAC operates under Minn. Stat. §144.053 (health data research authority).
  • Ethics review: determined exempt from IRB review as public health surveillance under federal regulations.
